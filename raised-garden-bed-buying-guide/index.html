How to choose the right raised garden bed

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best raised garden bed for 2026 balances material durability, depth for your plants, and your local climate. For most vegetables, look for beds at least 11–12 inches deep made from rot-resistant wood, galvanized steel, or composite lumber, with a bottomless design and good drainage. Prioritize beds that are easy to assemble and wide enough to reach the center from both sides.

What is the best material for a raised garden bed?

The three most common materials–wood, metal, and composite–each have clear trade-offs. Untreated cedar or redwood naturally resists rot and insects, lasting 7–10 years without chemicals, but costs more than pressure-treated pine. If you choose treated wood, look for ACQ or CA-C treated lumber made for garden use; older CCA treatments are no longer sold for residential beds. Galvanized steel beds have grown popular because they last 15–20 years, don’t rot, and heat up in sun to extend the growing season (though you may need to water more often). They come in modular kits that are simple to assemble but can dent if bumped. Composite lumber–a blend of recycled wood fibers and plastic–never rots or splinters, but it’s heavy, expensive, and doesn’t add nutrients to the soil. For most home gardeners, a mid-depth galvanized steel bed or a cedar bed offers the best value. Avoid thin plastic beds that crack under UV exposure; thick, food-grade polypropylene can work but often costs as much as wood.

How deep should a raised garden bed be?

Depth determines what you can grow. For shallow-rooted crops like lettuce, spinach, or herbs, a bed just 6–8 inches deep is enough–but only if the ground underneath is loose and not compacted. For tomatoes, peppers, carrots, or potatoes, aim for at least 11–12 inches of soil. A bed that sits directly on native soil lets roots grow deeper than the bed itself, so the depth measurement matters less than the total accessible soil. If you place the bed on concrete, patio, or poor soil, you need the full root zone inside the bed. For those situations, choose a bed 18 inches or taller for tomatoes and deep-rooted vegetables. Taller beds also reduce bending–a nice bonus for older gardeners or anyone with back issues. Just remember that taller beds require much more soil to fill, which adds to the upfront cost.

Should I choose a raised bed with or without a bottom?

Bottomless beds are the standard for in-ground gardening because they allow roots to reach native soil, improve drainage, and let earthworms and beneficial microbes move freely. This design also prevents water from pooling inside and keeps the soil from getting soggy. Most experienced gardeners prefer bottomless beds placed directly on leveled ground, often after removing grass or a thin layer of weeds. Beds with a solid bottom or a built-in liner are useful only when you need to protect a patio, deck, or rooftop from soil and moisture. Some kits include a wire mesh bottom to block burrowing pests like gophers or voles while still letting water drain. If you have serious pest pressure, look for a bed that offers a pest-proof mesh floor that can be removed later. Otherwise, skip the bottom and save money.

Where should I place my raised garden bed?

Sunlight is the single biggest factor. Most vegetables need at least 6–8 hours of direct sun each day. Observe your yard across the seasons: a spot that is sunny in spring may be shaded by a tree canopy in summer. Morning sun is gentler than harsh afternoon sun, but full-day sun is ideal for tomatoes, peppers, and squash. Leafy greens can tolerate a bit of shade, but even they grow better with more light. Level ground is critical to prevent uneven water pooling and structural stress. Before assembly, use a shovel to roughly level the area, then check with a carpenter’s level. If the bed is on a gentle slope, you can terrace two beds rather than trying to level a large area. Also consider access: leave enough space around the bed (at least 2 feet) to walk, kneel, or wheel a garden cart. Position the width so you can reach the center from both sides–typically 3 to 4 feet wide for a two-sided bed.

How do I ensure good drainage in a raised bed?

Good drainage starts with the bed itself. Bottomless beds on native soil usually drain well as long as the ground below isn’t compacted clay. If your soil is heavy clay, dig down 6–8 inches and mix in coarse sand or compost before placing the bed. For beds on hard surfaces, elevate the bed slightly to allow water to escape–many metal beds have a gap between the rim and the base. Inside the bed, avoid the old advice of putting gravel or rocks at the bottom. That creates a perched water table that can actually keep soil soggy. Instead, fill the entire bed with loose, high-quality garden soil mixed with compost. Add a 2-inch layer of organic mulch on top to slow evaporation and prevent crusting. If after rain the bed stays wet for more than a day, drill extra drainage holes in the sides (for wood or composite beds) or lift the bed on a few bricks.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best wood for a raised garden bed?

Western red cedar and redwood are naturally rot-resistant and safe for vegetables, typically lasting 7–10 years. Pressure-treated pine treated with ACQ or CA-C is now considered safe by most experts and costs roughly half as much, but it may last only 5–8 years. Avoid using railroad ties or old treated lumber from before 2004.

Do I need to line the inside of a raised bed?

You don’t usually need a liner for wood beds, but a layer of landscape fabric helps keep soil from washing out through gaps. For metal beds, a liner can prevent direct contact between the soil and galvanized steel (not toxic, but some plants are sensitive to zinc). Skip plastic liners because they trap moisture and rot wood faster.

How much soil do I need to fill a raised bed?

Calculate the volume by multiplying length × width × depth (in feet), then divide by 27 to get cubic yards. Standard 4×8-foot beds that are 12 inches deep need about 1.2 cubic yards of soil mix. For large beds, buying bulk soil from a landscape supplier is usually much cheaper than bagged soil from a garden center.

Can I use regular garden soil in a raised bed?

Regular native garden soil is too dense and may compact in a raised bed. Better options are a 50/50 blend of topsoil and high-quality compost, or a commercial raised-bed mix that includes peat moss or coconut coir for aeration. Avoid potting mix, which is too light and dries out quickly in a large bed.

How wide should a raised garden bed be?

A good rule is no wider than 4 feet if you can access both sides, or 3 feet if the bed is against a fence or wall. This allows you to comfortably reach the center without stepping into the soil. Length is flexible; common lengths are 6, 8, or 12 feet, but longer beds may need additional reinforcement in the middle.

Do raised beds need to be replaced over time?

Yes. Every material has a lifespan: untreated pine might last 3–5 years, cedar 7–10, composite lumber 20+, and galvanized steel 15–20 years if powder-coated. Moisture, ground contact, and sun exposure will all shorten that. Watch for warping, cracks, or rust around the bottom edges, and plan to replace when structural integrity fails.

In shortChoosing the right raised garden bed comes down to matching the material, depth, and placement to your specific soil, sunlight, and plants. A simple 4×8-foot cedar bed at 12 inches deep remains the most practical and affordable starting point for most beginners. Skip unnecessary extras like bottoms or plastic liners, invest in good soil, and you’ll have a bed that produces reliably for years.
